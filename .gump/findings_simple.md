## What makes music good?

**Simple:** Music is good when your body wants to move and your brain keeps guessing right about what comes next — but not every time.

**Why:** Your brain is always predicting the next sound. When it guesses right, that feels comfortable. When it guesses wrong but the surprise makes sense after, that feels amazing. Too many right guesses = boring. Too many wrong guesses = noise. The sweet spot in between = music.

---

## Why do some sounds go together?

**Simple:** Sounds that vibrate at simple ratios (2:1, 3:2, 4:3) fit together. Sounds at messy ratios fight each other.

**Why:** A guitar string vibrating at 200 Hz and another at 300 Hz (ratio 3:2) share overlapping vibration patterns. They reinforce each other. A string at 200 Hz and one at 287 Hz don't share patterns. They create friction. Your ear hears the reinforcement as "nice" and the friction as "tense." Both are useful. Neither is wrong.

## Why does rhythm feel good?

**Simple:** Your body already has rhythms — heartbeat, breathing, walking. Music that lines up with those rhythms feels like home.

**Why:** Your heart beats about 70 times per minute. You walk at about 120 steps per minute. Your favorite songs are probably around 80-130 BPM. That's not a coincidence. Your brain links sound rhythms to body rhythms automatically.

## What makes a drum machine sound dead and a drummer sound alive?

**Simple:** A machine hits exactly on time. A human hits 10-30 milliseconds early or late, and each deviation is connected to the one before it. That connected imperfection is called 1/f noise. Your heartbeat has it. Ocean waves have it. Everything alive has it.

**Why:** Your ear evolved to detect living things (predators, prey, tribe members). The signature of life is not perfection — it's correlated imperfection. A machine's perfect timing literally sounds dead to your ear because nothing alive moves that way.

**In GUMP:** Every drum hit now has 1/f timing offsets. The drums are alive.

## Why does "I love you" sound the same in every language?

**Simple:** In 10 out of 14 languages tested, "I love you" follows the same shape: voice goes up, peaks on the word "love," then comes down. An arch.

**Why:** It's the shape of a breath. Inhale (gathering), peak (expressing), exhale (releasing). You can only say love the way you breathe. The peak is always on the verb — the ACT of loving — because that's where the body puts its energy.

**In GUMP:** This arch is the natural melodic seed. Rise, arrive, depart. Scale degrees 3→5→1.

## Does weather actually change music?

**Simple:** Yes. Warm places make wider, more open sounds. Cold places make tighter, compressed sounds. This is proven across 9,000+ languages.

**Why:** Cold air dries your vocal cords. Dry vocal cords can't make precise pitch changes. So cold-climate languages evolved fewer pitch variations. Warm, humid air keeps vocal cords elastic. So tropical languages have more tones, wider melodies, more open vowels.

**In GUMP:** Today's temperature widens or compresses the pitch intervals. Not "rain = sad." Rain = how your body makes sound in wet air.

## What's the difference between a hit song and a non-hit?

**Simple:** Mostly luck and algorithms. But there IS a quality threshold. Songs that cross it have: a clear beat you can move to, a melody that's surprising but makes sense, and sound quality that feels "right" on any speaker.

**Why:** The top 1% of songs getting 90% of streams is a power law — the same math as city sizes and wealth distribution. The concentration comes from the system (playlists, algorithms, social sharing), not from the songs being 90x better. But bad songs can't enter the loop. The sound gets you to the threshold. The system does the rest.

**What nobody has studied:** How the tension changes second by second WITHIN a song. Every study analyzed averages. But music isn't an average — it's a flow. The universal pattern might be in the SHAPE of how tension rises and falls proportionally from beginning to end, regardless of the song's length.

## Why do two people near each other start moving in sync?

**Simple:** Your brain copies what it sees. When you watch someone move, the part of your brain that controls movement fires as if YOU were moving that way. Within one second, your timing shifts toward theirs. You don't choose this. It just happens.

**Why:** Synchronized movement releases bonding chemicals (oxytocin, endorphins). Humans evolved to sync because coordinated groups survive better. The drum circle works across every culture because it's biology, not tradition.

**In GUMP (Outfits):** Two people with phones near each other are already musically coupled through their nervous systems before the phones even talk. The devices just make the connection audible.

## What is the void drone?

**Simple:** When you stop moving, GUMP doesn't go silent. There's a deep, barely-audible hum that breathes in and out every 10 seconds.

**Why:** Your cardiovascular system resonates at 0.1 Hz — one cycle every 10 seconds. Breathing at this rate strengthens the nerve that connects your heart to your brain and produces calm. The void drone breathes at your body's own resting frequency.

**In GUMP:** Stillness isn't leaving the music. It's arriving at the deepest note your body already plays.
