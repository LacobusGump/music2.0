The Line

Two stone arks. One sea. Same direction.

drag to pan · the gold line is the Camino del Sureste (Alicante → Santiago)

The Codex Calixtinus (1139 AD) is the Book of St. James. A medieval guidebook that tells pilgrims: walk from HERE to THERE to find the stone ark that came from the east in a boat.

The Camino del Sureste starts at Alicante on the southeastern Mediterranean coast of Spain. It crosses the entire country northwest to Santiago de Compostela in Galicia, where the stone sarcophagus of the apostle James supposedly arrived by sea from Palestine.

82 km south of Alicante, off the coast of Cabo de Palos, the basalt sarcophagus of Pharaoh Menkaure sits on the seabed at 30–50 meters depth. It left Alexandria on the ship Beatrice in September 1838. Last spotted off Cartagena. Sank within sight of the coast. Basalt. Decorated. Palace facade. 186 years underwater. Never recovered.

Two stone arks. Both from the eastern Mediterranean. Both sailed west to Spain. One is a legend that built the biggest pilgrimage in European history. The other is a historical fact sitting in 40 meters of water.

The apostle James was one of the inner three. First apostle killed (44 AD, by Herod Agrippa). Tradition says he preached in Spain before returning to Jerusalem to die. His disciples put his body in a stone ark — the Arca Marmórica — and set it in a boat from Palestine. It arrived at Padrón, Galicia. The town is named after the stone (pedrón).

The Codex Calixtinus (1139) tells the story in Book III: the transfer of the apostle’s body to Compostela. Book V is the pilgrim’s guide — where to walk, where to stop, which relics to visit. 900 years of pilgrims followed the path. Millions of feet. All walking toward the stone that came from the east.

The sarcophagus of Menkaure was pulled from the smallest pyramid by Colonel Vyse in 1837. Basalt. Decorated with a palace facade pattern (the earliest known). Put on the merchant ship Beatrice at Alexandria. Left September 20, 1838. Stopped in Malta. Departed October 13. Spotted off Cartagena. Never seen again.

The lid was sent on a separate ship and arrived safely. It’s in the British Museum. The box is on the seabed off Cabo de Palos.
