Design the optimal system

The Optimizer measures waste. This tool does the reverse: it designs the cheapest possible system. For every operation in your pipeline, there is a choice. Erase the bits (pay energy) or keep them (pay memory). The optimal design picks the cheaper path for each block.

The Landauer limit isn’t just a floor. It’s a design constraint. Every bit you choose NOT to erase is energy you choose NOT to spend. The question isn’t “how fast?” It’s “which bits can I keep?”

The physics

Every computation has two paths. The irreversible path erases bits, costing at least kT ln 2 per bit (Landauer, 1961). The reversible path keeps all intermediate states, costing zero energy but requiring memory overhead of roughly 2× the input size (Bennett, 1973).

The optimal design minimizes total cost = energy cost + memory cost. Today, with silicon at ~106× the Landauer limit, irreversible is almost always cheaper — energy is cheap relative to memory. But as chips approach the limit, memory becomes the cheaper resource. The crossover is coming. This tool shows you when.

Koomey’s law observes that the energy efficiency of computation roughly doubles every 2 years. As the inefficiency factor drops from 106 toward 103 toward 1, operations that were once cheap to run irreversibly become candidates for reversal. The Architect computes this crossover for each operation type.
