Quantum caveat
Shor's algorithm on a quantum computer could factor in polynomial time. Quantum operations can be thermodynamically reversible, so the Landauer floor does not apply to quantum factoring in the same way. This calculator shows the classical floor — the minimum energy any classical computer would need, no matter how perfectly built.

Landauer's principle

In 1961, Rolf Landauer proved that erasing one bit of information in a computation must dissipate at least kT ln 2 of energy as heat, where k is Boltzmann's constant and T is temperature. This is not an engineering limit — it is a consequence of the second law of thermodynamics. Any irreversible logical operation (AND, OR, ERASE) destroys information and pays this cost.

In 2012, Bérut et al. experimentally confirmed the Landauer limit by trapping a colloidal particle in a double-well potential and measuring the heat dissipated when erasing one bit. The measured value matched kT ln 2 to within experimental error.

The General Number Field Sieve (GNFS) is the fastest known classical algorithm for factoring large integers. Its time complexity for an n-bit number is approximately exp(c · n1/3 · (ln n)2/3) where c ≈ 1.923. Each of these operations requires at least one irreversible bit erasure. Multiplying gives the thermodynamic floor.
