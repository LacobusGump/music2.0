The hermit thrush has been singing for 66 million years. Humans arrived 200,000 years ago.

Its song uses intervals drawn from the overtone series — the same mathematical ratios that define musical harmony across every human culture.

Doolittle, E.L., et al. (2014). "Overtone-based pitch selection in hermit thrush song." PNAS, 111(46).

What every culture found

Across hundreds of recordings from societies worldwide, researchers found the same acoustic features appearing independently: discrete pitches, a steady beat, repetition, and octave equivalence. A separate study catalogued music from 315 cultures and found the same four functions everywhere: dance, love, healing, and lullabies.

Nobody coordinated. These things emerged everywhere on their own.

Savage, P.E., et al. (2015). "Statistical universals reveal the structures and functions of human music." PNAS, 112(29).  |  Mehr, S.A., et al. (2019). "Universality and diversity in human song." Science, 366(6468).

The only universal interval

Researchers played Western "consonant" and "dissonant" chords to the Tsimane of the Bolivian Amazon — a group with no exposure to Western music. They rated both as equally pleasant. Consonance preference is at least partly learned.

The one interval every group recognized as special: the octave — a 2:1 frequency ratio. Two notes. One sound.

Rhythm came from the body

A culture's spoken language shapes its music. Instrumental rhythms mirror speech stress patterns, even with no words present.

Bulgarian musicians don't count 7/8. They speak it: "apple apple gallery."

Two people near each other are already making music

When you watch someone move, the mirror system in your brain fires as if you were moving. Within one second, your timing shifts toward theirs — without trying.

Two strangers in rocking chairs tend to coordinate their rhythm. No one decides to. The nervous system does it on its own.

drifting apart

Watanabe, K. (2007). Cognition.  |  Richardson, M.J. & Schmidt, R.C. (2007). Human Movement Science.

The alive sound

The difference between a drum machine and a drummer is 10 to 30 milliseconds of timing variation. Not random — correlated. Each deviation connects to the one before it. This is called 1/f noise.

It appears in heartbeats, ocean waves, and neural firing. Your ear is remarkably sensitive to this pattern — it's what makes something sound alive.

Stillness is not silence

Your cardiovascular system has a resonant frequency at 0.1 Hz — one full cycle every ten seconds. Breathing at this rate strengthens the baroreflex, the feedback loop between heart and brain, and produces measurable physiological calm.

When you stop moving, you are not leaving the music. You are arriving at the deepest rhythm your body already plays.

Climate shapes the voice

Across 9,179 language samples, warm humid air keeps vocal cords elastic, producing wider pitch intervals. Cold dry air stiffens them, compressing speech into narrower ranges.

Music from warm places sounds different from music from cold places because the bodies making it are physically different in that air.

There may be much more to discover

Birds sang before us. 315 isolated cultures found the same four things. Two strangers synchronize without trying. The alive sound and the dead sound differ by 15 milliseconds.

What if music isn't entertainment? What if it's a fundamental property of living systems — as basic as breathing, as automatic as a heartbeat? How much of the human experience is music we haven't learned to hear yet?

We're building an instrument to find out.

Your body is already making music. We are turning up the volume.
