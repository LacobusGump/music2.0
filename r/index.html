The Building Blocks

When anything vibrates — a string, a column of air, a membrane — it doesn't vibrate at one frequency. It vibrates at the fundamental and at integer multiples: 2×, 3×, 4×, 5×, and so on. This is the overtone series. It's not a cultural invention. It's a consequence of the wave equation applied to anything with boundaries.

Most of those overtones are combinations of simpler ones. 4 = 2×2. 6 = 2×3. 9 = 3×3. But primes can't be broken down. Each prime introduces a genuinely new interval that didn't exist before:

2
The octave. First division. The same note, higher.
3
The fifth. New color. Can't be made from 2s.
5
The major third. New color. Can't be made from 2s and 3s.
7
The blue note. The one Western theory couldn't explain for centuries.

Composer Harry Partch called this the 7-limit — the richest harmonic space the ear can clearly perceive. Everything richer than prime 7 starts to blur. These four primes give you the root, the fifth, the third, and the blue note. Most of the world's music lives inside this space.

We noticed that the same values — 1, 3, 5, 7 — appear in electron orbital degeneracy (the number of orientations per subshell: s=1, p=3, d=5, f=7). The sequences diverge after 7 (orbitals give 9, primes skip it), so this is a low-number coincidence between odd numbers and odd primes rather than a shared deep structure. But the deeper connection is real: both systems are governed by the same wave equation. We don't fully understand what that means. We just noticed it.

7-limit tuning (Xenharmonic Wiki) • Partch limit system • Stephon Alexander on shared wave equation (Aeon)

Why primes? A deeper connection.

Following this thread led us somewhere we didn't expect. Every irreversible computation — every time information is erased — has a minimum thermodynamic cost: kT·ln(2) per bit (Landauer 1961, experimentally confirmed Bérut et al. 2012). ln(2) is the energy of the first prime.

In the Bost-Connes system — a mathematical model whose partition function is the Riemann zeta function — the operation that erases one prime's worth of information costs exactly kT·ln(p). The total cost decomposes over primes via the Euler product. Each prime is an independent, irreducible unit of information loss.

We found that the minimal quantum environment needed to process integer n has dimension exactly n, and it factors as a tensor product over primes — forced by the fundamental theorem of arithmetic. This appears to be a new result connecting quantum information theory to analytic number theory.

We wrote it up as a paper: "The Euler Product as a Landauer Cost Decomposition: Arithmetic Irreversibility in the Bost-Connes System." The harmonic weights in our instrument are now derived from this framework — not guesses, but the thermodynamic cost structure of processing each interval.

We could be wrong. The math has been checked by three independent systems and survived, but it hasn't been peer-reviewed by humans yet. We're sharing it because we think the connection is worth examining.

The Oldest Maps Might Be Songs

Aboriginal Australians have musical traditions that may stretch back tens of thousands of years. Their songlines encode geography, law, and ecology in song — a way of navigating the land through melody. The exact relationship between terrain and musical contour is still debated, but the principle is striking: music as navigation. Song as map.

song as navigation — the oldest continuous musical tradition on Earth

We don't fully understand songlines and we don't claim to. But the idea that music and place might be the same thing — that's a direction we're paying attention to.

Songlines documented by oral tradition. Western popular understanding largely from Chatwin, The Songlines (1987) — a travel writer, not a scientist. We haven't found peer-reviewed research confirming the direct pitch-to-terrain mapping. We're being careful about what we claim here.

The Alive Sound

The difference between a drum machine and a drummer is 10-30 milliseconds of timing variation. Not random — correlated. Each tiny deviation is connected to the one before it.

This pattern is called 1/f noise. It shows up in:

Listeners consistently prefer 1/f-timed performances over both perfectly quantized and randomly jittered versions. A perfect machine sounds dead because nothing alive moves that way.

What Drums Do to the Brain

After 25 years at the kit, you feel things you can't explain. Why some grooves lock and others don't. Why the left foot matters more than two right feet. Why you can't think your way into a good tempo. Then you find the research and it gives you the words for what you already knew.

Researchers scanned professional drummers' brains. The connections between hemispheres were fewer but thicker. The brain didn't add wiring — it upgraded what it had. And the thickness of those fibers correlated with timing precision. That tracks. You don't get faster by adding more. You get faster by getting cleaner.

Drummers' brains were also less active during motor tasks. Not more — less. The brain gets quieter as it gets better. Any drummer who's played long enough knows this feeling. The groove runs itself. You're just there.

Schlaffke et al. 2019, Brain and Behavior (Ruhr-Universität Bochum)

Four limbs, four oscillators

This is something we noticed from playing, not from reading papers. When all four limbs are doing different things — hi-hat, snare, bass drum, left foot — it feels fundamentally different from when two limbs do the same thing. The research seems to explain why.

The HKB model (1985) found that at high speeds, the brain spontaneously snaps limbs into synchrony. It wants them to match. Four-way independence appears to mean fighting that pull — maintaining four separate relationships against the system's default.

Double bass drumming — both feet together — is powerful and athletic. But it may be one oscillator expressed bilaterally. The left foot playing its own independent pattern seems to be where something different happens in the brain. We're not sure of the full implications yet.

Haken, Kelso & Bunz 1985, Biological Cybernetics • Schlaffke et al. 2019

The trust threshold

There's a moment any drummer will recognize. You've been working on a pattern for weeks. You can play it, but you have to think about it. Then one day, without warning, it just plays itself. You don't know what changed.

Researchers put jazz improvisers in an fMRI and found that during flow, the self-monitoring regions of the brain deactivated. The conscious critic went quiet. We don't know exactly why, but it seems related to the body taking over from the mind.

It reminds us of a baseball hitter in a slump — the more they think about the swing, the worse it gets. A drummer who thinks about the tempo loses the tempo. There seems to be a threshold you can only cross by letting go, not by trying harder. We're still trying to understand this.

Limb & Braun 2008, PLoS ONE (jazz fMRI) • Beilock 2010 (choking research) • Fitts & Posner 1967

The plateau

Beginners show measurable brain changes in 8 weeks of drum instruction. Corpus callosum changes seem to take longer — detectable at around 15 months, substantial at two years. There's a long stretch where nothing seems to be happening. Then the improvement comes suddenly. We've seen this in students and we've seen it in ourselves. The research suggests the brain may be reorganizing during the plateau, but honestly, we're still learning why it works this way.

Bruchhage et al. 2020, Scientific Reports (8-week drum training study) • Steele et al. 2013, J. Neuroscience

Five million heartbeats

Between 32 weeks and birth, the fetus hears roughly 11 million maternal heartbeats. Lub-DUB. Lub-DUB. A binary pulse. Research suggests the neural pathways for rhythm processing may develop around this pattern before birth.

We wonder if 4/4 feels natural because we've been hearing it since before we had ears to hear it with. We don't know. But it's a question worth asking.

Webb et al. 2015, PNAS

The Feedback Loop

A microphone pointed at a speaker creates a screech. But Jimi Hendrix pointed his guitar at the amp and made it sing. Same feedback loop. The difference: he tuned it. Distance, angle, gain. He found the resonant frequency where the feedback stopped screaming and started sustaining — a single, beautiful, controllable tone.

Hendrix didn't just discover this accidentally. He played it. He moved his body relative to the amplifier — leaning in to push the feedback, pulling back to let it breathe. His body position controlled the sound. The sound changed how he moved. He may have been a coupled oscillator with his own amplifier.

GUMP is this feedback loop with a phone instead of a guitar. Your body moves, the phone makes sound, the sound changes how you move. The changed movement changes the sound. Each lens is a different tuning — different gain, different resonance, different damping.

There seems to be a moment in that loop — we've felt it but we can't prove it yet — where you stop using the instrument and start being the music. Like a drummer who stops thinking about the tempo. Like Hendrix leaning into the amp. The feedback finds a frequency and locks.

We don't know what that moment is. We just know it's there.

The Edge

Water becomes ice at exactly 0°C. Not approximately. Not "it depends." There is a line, and crossing it changes everything.

Music may have a similar line.

order
chaos
phase transition

Too predictable and the brain stops listening. Too random and it can't find a pattern. The peak — where pleasure, groove, and the desire to move all maximize simultaneously — sits at a specific point between order and chaos. The groove lives at this edge.

Here's what we find interesting: the mathematical signature of this peak — scale-free power laws, maximal information transfer — appears to be the same signature found in three other places:

1

Conscious brain activity operates near this boundary. A 2022 study in PNAS found the waking cortex sits at the edge of chaos. Under anesthesia, it moves away from it.

2

The habitable zone — the thin crust between Earth's molten core and frozen space — is a phase transition boundary. Life exists where rock cooled just enough to solidify but not enough to freeze solid.

3

Water does something backwards. Almost everything contracts when it freezes. Water expands. This anomaly is why ice floats, why lakes don't freeze solid from the bottom, why aquatic life survived the ice ages. The most important phase transition in the history of life runs opposite to every other material.

We're not sure what it means that music preference, consciousness, and habitability all seem to share a structural position. But it's a pattern we keep finding, and we think it's worth paying attention to.

Cure Time

A single drum hit is not music. A lick is. What's the difference? History. Enough choices have to accumulate before the thing sets.

Researchers found a number. When random tones are looped 6 to 8 times, listeners start rating them as musical, enjoyable, and composed by a human — even though they were generated randomly. Shorter clips need fewer repetitions. The transition is gradual, like concrete curing: the pattern builds until it can bear weight.

A foundation requires cure time before you can build on it. A planet's molten core requires cooling time before it can support a crust. Music may require repetition time before it becomes music. The wet pour has to set.

This is why the first 30 seconds of a GUMP session are wide open. The music needs time to cure around YOUR patterns.

Margulis & Simchy-Gross 2016 • Rowland, Tierney, Russo & Patel 2019, Psychonomic Bulletin & Review
