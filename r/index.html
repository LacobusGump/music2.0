The Tsimane Discovery

MIT researchers traveled to the Bolivian Amazon to study the Tsimane — a group with zero exposure to Western music.

They played them "pleasant" chords and "harsh" chords.

The Tsimane rated them as equally pleasant.

Consonance is not a law of nature. It's a learned preference. The only interval the Tsimane recognized as special was the octave — 2:1 ratio. That one is in the ear itself, not in the culture.

McDermott, Schultz, Undurraga & Godoy, 2016, Nature

The Signature of Life

The difference between a drum machine and a drummer is 10-30 milliseconds of timing variation. Not random — correlated. Each tiny deviation connected to the one before it.

This pattern is called 1/f noise. It appears in:

alive — correlated imperfection
heartbeats • ocean waves • neural firing • great drummers
dead — perfect timing
drum machines

Your ear evolved to detect this as the signature of something alive. A perfect machine sounds dead because nothing alive moves that way.

Hennig et al. 2011, PLoS ONE • Butterfield 2010

Two People Near Each Other Are Already Making Music

Three proven mechanisms:

1

Mirror Neurons

Seeing someone move activates your motor cortex as if you were moving. Measured via EEG. The brain copies what it sees.

2

Speed Contagion

Watching someone move at a certain speed shifts your timing toward theirs. Within one second. Unconsciously.

3

The Rocking Chair Effect

Two strangers in rocking chairs next to each other spontaneously synchronize. Not trying. Not aware. The physics does it.

By the time GUMP phones exchange data, the bodies are already coupled. The devices don't create the connection. They make it audible.

Watanabe 2007 • Richardson & Schmidt 2007 • Lindenberger et al. 2009
