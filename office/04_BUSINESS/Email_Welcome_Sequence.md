Three days ago you signed up. Hopefully you've moved with GUMP at least once by now.

I want to tell you what I'm actually building.

Right now GUMP is a solo instrument. You, your body, your phone. But the reason I've been working on this for 15 years isn't the solo version.

It's what happens when two people are playing at the same time and they start to approach each other.

Imagine: you're in a room. You can't see someone coming around the corner — but you can hear them. Their sound arrives before they do. A harmonic presence that gets louder, richer, more defined the closer they get. At 5 feet, your rhythms start to sync — not because you're trying to, but because that's what coupled oscillators do. Pendulum clocks on the same wall sync without touching. You will too.

This is called Music Outfits. And it's next.

Silent discos where strangers walk toward each other and make music without talking. Kids on a playground, their bodies making music together without knowing why. GPS music that changes every time you turn a corner.

Your body is going to be recognizable by its sound. People will start to know you by how you sound coming down the hall.

This is the vision. You're in early.

The instrument now has water bottle physics. That means when you tilt the phone, the pitch doesn't jump — it sloshes. It has momentum, weight, and a bounce when it hits the wall. If you tilt fast and stop hard, you'll hear a shaker hit at the extreme. That's the water hitting the side of the bottle.

It sounds small. It doesn't feel small. The difference between a tilt that feels mechanical and a tilt that feels physical is everything.
