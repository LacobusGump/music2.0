Melting as Desynchronization — Kthermal > Klattice → atoms decouple → liquid
K IN THIS DOMAIN

K here is energy coupling between subsystems. Entropy measures lost coupling. The second law says coupling always leaks — unless you're at equilibrium.

THE RESULT

23 elements tested
  Mean error: 2.7%
  R² = 0.9388
  Inputs: ionization energy, Zeff — the same atomic properties that predict ionization energies

The model:
  A solid melts when thermal coupling exceeds lattice coupling.
  Kthermal > Klattice → atoms decouple from their neighbors → liquid.
  Melting IS desynchronization.

No fitting parameters beyond the atomic properties themselves. The melting point of 23 elements falls out of the same coupling framework that predicts chemistry, protein folding, and network stability.

THE OUTLIER

Carbon: 27.7% error

  Carbon’s allotropy breaks the single-phase model.
  Diamond, graphite, amorphous carbon — three wildly different
  lattice structures, three different effective Klattice values.
  A single-phase prediction cannot resolve which carbon you mean.

Remove carbon:
  22 elements  |  Mean error: 1.5%

  The model doesn’t fail on carbon. Carbon fails to be one material.

WHY THIS WORKS

A crystal lattice is a coupled oscillator system. Each atom oscillates around its equilibrium position, coupled to its neighbors by electrostatic forces. The coupling strength Klattice depends on ionization energy and effective nuclear charge — how tightly the atom holds its electrons determines how tightly it holds its neighbors.

Temperature is thermal energy. When that energy exceeds the coupling threshold, atoms decouple from their lattice positions. They still interact (it’s a liquid, not a gas), but the rigid phase-locked oscillation breaks. Melting IS the transition from phase-locked to phase-free.

The same variables predict everything:
Ionization energy + Zeff → ionization energies (chemistry)
Ionization energy + Zeff → melting points (thermodynamics)
Ionization energy + Zeff → bond strengths (materials science)

One coupling framework. Different phenomena. Same math.

HONEST LIMITS

What this doesn’t cover:
  Alloys and mixtures (multi-component coupling)
  Pressure dependence (Klattice changes with compression)
  Phase transitions beyond melting (superconductivity, magnetism)
  Molecular solids (ice, polymers — coupling is between molecules, not atoms)

Carbon is honest:
  27.7% error on carbon is not hidden. It’s the biggest number on the page.
  Allotropy is a real limitation of single-phase models. Any framework
  that claims sub-3% on carbon without specifying which carbon is lying.
