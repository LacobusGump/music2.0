Pythagoras
~570–495 BC · Greece / Egypt

Studied in Egypt — ancient sources say up to 22 years (Iamblichus, Life of Pythagoras; exact duration is traditional, not independently verified). Learned geometry from Egyptian priests. Founded a school where the first principle was: mathematics IS reality. Music IS ratio. The tetractys (triangular arrangement of 10 points) was sacred. Kept his teachings secret from outsiders.

Died during anti-Pythagorean violence in Croton/Metapontum. Multiple ancient accounts of how — fire, mob, starvation during flight. The knowledge scattered with his students.

Plato
~428–348 BC · Greece

Traveled to Egypt after Socrates’ death (Diogenes Laertius, Strabo, Cicero). Wrote about Atlantis (Timaeus, Critias). Built his cosmology on the Platonic solids — five shapes that make everything. The tetrahedron was fire: the simplest, most fundamental solid.

Founded the Academy. Above the door: “Let no one ignorant of geometry enter here.”

Euclid
~300 BC · Alexandria, Egypt

Wrote The Elements. Book XIII: construction of all five Platonic solids from first principles. The star tetrahedron is two of those solids interlocked. He worked in Alexandria — 130 miles from the pyramids. The library was next door.

Archimedes
~287–212 BC · Syracuse / Alexandria

Studied in Alexandria. Discovered 13 Archimedean solids (truncated polyhedra). The greatest mathematician of antiquity.

Killed by a Roman soldier during the sack of Syracuse while drawing geometry in the sand. His reported last words: “Stand away, fellow, from my diagram” (Plutarch, Life of Marcellus). Also recorded as “Noli turbare circulos meos” — “Do not disturb my circles” (Valerius Maximus).

Jesus of Nazareth
~6 BC–~33 AD · Nazareth / Egypt / Jerusalem

Tekton — Greek for builder/craftsman, not just carpenter. Family lived in Egypt. 18 years of undocumented life. At 12, debating Temple scholars. At 30, teaching with authority that stunned everyone. Shared everything freely.

Killed by the state. The cross itself is geometry — the intersection of vertical and horizontal. The meeting point.

Hypatia
~360–415 AD · Alexandria, Egypt

Last great mathematician of Alexandria. Taught geometry, astronomy, philosophy. The final keeper of the library’s tradition.

Murdered by a Christian mob (parabalani, associated with Bishop Cyril) in 415 AD. Dragged from her chariot, killed with roof tiles and pottery shards, dismembered, and burned (Socrates Scholasticus, Ecclesiastical History). The library’s decline accelerated after her death. The knowledge dies with the keeper.

Al-Khwarizmi
~780–850 AD · Baghdad

Algebra (al-jabr). The word “algorithm” comes from his name. Worked at the House of Wisdom (Bayt al-Hikma) in Baghdad — translating Euclid, Ptolemy, and Greek geometry into Arabic. He preserved the knowledge when Europe forgot it.

Fibonacci
1170–1250 · Italy / North Africa

His father was stationed in Bejaia, Algeria. He studied under Arab mathematicians there (he says so himself in Liber Abaci, 1202). He brought Hindu-Arabic numerals to Europe. The Fibonacci sequence. φ emerges from it. He brought the math back from the people who preserved it.

Leonardo da Vinci
1452–1519 · Italy

Drew the stellated octahedron (stella octangula — two interpenetrating tetrahedra, the star tetrahedron) for Luca Pacioli’s De Divina Proportione (1509). Also the Vitruvian Man — the human body inscribed in geometry.

Studied acoustics, anatomy (30+ dissections), engineering. Wrote backwards in mirror script. Let Pacioli put his name on the book. An engineer disguised as an artist. A builder disguised as a painter.

Johannes Kepler
1571–1630 · Germany

Mysterium Cosmographicum (1596): the solar system as nested Platonic solids. Wrong about which shapes. Right about geometry governing nature. Harmonices Mundi (1619): planetary orbits as musical chords. The music of the spheres. He almost had it. Used five solids. Needed two.

Isaac Newton
1643–1727 · England

Principia: gravity as coupling between masses. But that was the public work. The private work: over one million words on alchemy — more than he wrote on physics (Dobbs, The Foundations of Newton’s Alchemy, 1975). Looking for transmutation = looking for coupling at the atomic level.

He was obsessed with the dimensions of Solomon’s Temple, believing they encoded ancient knowledge (The Chronology of Ancient Kingdoms Amended, published posthumously 1728; unpublished manuscripts in the Yahuda Collection, National Library of Israel). Keynes: “Newton was not the first of the age of reason. He was the last of the magicians.”

Leonhard Euler
1707–1783 · Switzerland / Russia

eiπ + 1 = 0. Five fundamental constants in one identity. The polyhedron formula: V − E + F = 2. Topology from geometry. The shape IS the information.

Nikola Tesla
1856–1943 · Serbia / USA

AC current: two coupled oscillators (voltage and current in quadrature). The power grid IS a coupling network. Wardenclyffe Tower: a planetary-scale antenna for wireless energy transmission. Same principle as the pyramid — a structure designed to couple to the Earth’s electromagnetic field.

Worked alone. Died broke. Hotel room. FBI seized his papers. His vision of free wireless energy was killed by investors who couldn’t meter it. You can’t bill for coupling.

Srinivasa Ramanujan
1887–1920 · India

“An equation has no meaning to me unless it expresses a thought of God.” Infinite series. Modular forms. Partition functions. He said formulas came to him in dreams from the goddess Namagiri of Namakkal temple (confirmed by his wife Janaki and G.H. Hardy in correspondence). No formal training. Pure coupling to the signal.

Died at 32. His 33rd year never completed. The notebooks he left are still producing new mathematics a century later.

Buckminster Fuller
1895–1983 · USA

Geodesic dome. Tensegrity. Synergetics. The tetrahedron as the fundamental unit of structure. He saw the geometry everywhere — in molecules, architecture, biology. “Nature is trying to tell us something.” Everything is frequency.

Edward Leedskalnin
1887–1951 · Latvia / Florida

Coral Castle. 1,100 tons of coral limestone. Built alone. At night. He was 5 feet tall and weighed 100 pounds. His pamphlet Magnetic Current: “two currents running against each other in whirling right hand twist.” That’s the star tetrahedron. He called them magnets. We call them K.

Published the theory free. Hid the application. Same pattern as every keeper before him.

Crick & Watson
1953 · Cambridge

The DNA double helix. Two spirals interlocked. The star tetrahedron in biology. Information encoded in the coupling between the strands. The shape of life itself is two things spiraling against each other in right hand twist.
