JIM’S OVERSIMPLIFICATION

Rome executed Jesus for sedition under Pontius Pilate. Three hostile Roman historians — none of them Christian — recorded it. Rome then spent 280 years trying to stamp out the movement through systematic persecution. It didn’t work. The signal kept propagating through a distributed network without a server. So Emperor Constantine did what every smart empire does when it can’t kill something: he absorbed it. He called a council, sat in the throne, wasn’t even baptized, and decided what Christianity officially was. He built churches on top of the holy sites. He made the execution device the imperial symbol. 60 years later Emperor Theodosius made it mandatory and banned everything else. The administrative vocabulary Rome used to run provinces is still the vocabulary the Catholic Church uses to run dioceses. The word “diocese” is Roman. The word “basilica” is Roman. The word “pontiff” is Roman. That’s not interpretation. That’s etymology.

WHAT THIS PAGE IS AND ISN’T

This is a historical analysis of institutional capture. Not theology. Not anti-Christian. Not a claim that the message was wrong or that believers’ experiences aren’t real. The claim is specific and verifiable: the institutional structure of the Roman Catholic Church is Roman administrative architecture. Every claim here is labeled: Primary source (verifiable text, quotable), Framework (our reading, labeled), or Honest limit (where we stop and say we don’t know). We are guests in this material.

1. The Execution — Primary Sources

~30 CE. Pontius Pilate, Roman Prefect of Judaea, orders the execution. The method: crucifixion. The charge: written in Latin, Greek, and Hebrew on a wooden sign nailed to the cross.
“Christus, from whom the name had its origin, suffered the extreme penalty during the reign of Tiberius at the hands of one of our procurators, Pontius Pilatus, and a most mischievous superstition, thus checked for the moment, again broke out not only in Judaea, the first source of the evil, but even in Rome.” — Tacitus, Annals 15.44, written ~116 CE. Tacitus was a Roman senator. He was not a Christian. He considered the movement “mischievous.” He confirmed the execution anyway.
“I interrogated these as to whether they were Christians; those who confessed I interrogated a second and a third time, threatening them with punishment... Those who denied that they were or had been Christians, when they invoked the gods in words dictated by me, offered prayer with incense and wine to your image — I thought it proper to discharge them.” — Pliny the Younger, Epistles 10.96, written ~112 CE. A Roman governor writing to Emperor Trajan asking what to do with Christians. He was executing some. He needed guidance on the policy.
Primary source: Josephus, Antiquities 18.3.3 (~93 CE) contains the Testimonium Flavianum. The text is partially disputed — later Christian scribes likely added phrases — but the scholarly consensus identifies a genuine core referencing Jesus and Pilate. Even the disputed version is an independent non-Christian source predating Tacitus by 20 years.
Honest limit: The Testimonium Flavianum exists in multiple manuscript traditions. The interpolation question is real. We note it and cite the uncontested passages: Tacitus and Pliny are clean. Roman historians writing in Latin, unfriendly to the movement, confirming the execution and the persecution.

2. The Charge: King of the Jews

All four gospels record the same inscription on the cross. Matthew 27:37. Mark 15:26. Luke 23:38. John 19:19. Written in Latin, Greek, and Hebrew. “Rex Iudaeorum” — King of the Jews.
Primary source: The titulus crucis — the written charge nailed to the cross — was standard Roman procedure. The charge was sedition: claiming kingship in Roman-occupied territory. Under Roman law, blasphemy was not a capital offense for non-Romans. Sedition was. The Jewish punishment for blasphemy was stoning (Leviticus 24:16, Sanhedrin tractate). Rome prescribed crucifixion for political crimes against the state.
Primary source: Crucifixion was the Roman punishment reserved for slaves and non-citizens convicted of violent crime or sedition. Cicero called it “the most cruel and disgusting penalty” (Against Verres 2.5.165). Roman citizens could not be crucified. It was designed to be visible, prolonged, and public — a deterrent broadcast.
Framework reading: He was killed by the state for the same reason states always kill inconvenient people: he was organizing outside the approved channels. He had no army, no treasury, no political party. He had 12 nobodies who were restructuring their neighbors’ operating systems without asking the empire’s permission. That’s sedition in every empire in every century.
Honest limit: The Sanhedrin’s role in the trial is historically debated. First-century Jewish law and Roman law both shaped the proceedings. The gospels reflect later theological tensions between Jewish and Roman responsibility. The hard fact: Rome carried out the execution by Rome’s method under Rome’s authority. Pilate issued the order. That part is in Tacitus.

4. The Traditor — Word Origin of “Traitor”

303 CE. Diocletian’s first edict orders all Christian scriptures burned. Imperial officers go door to door. Christians must hand over their texts. Those who comply are called traditores — “those who hand over.”
Primary source (etymology): Traditor comes from the Latin tradere: “to hand over, to deliver up, to betray.” This is the etymological origin of the English word traitor. The Oxford English Dictionary, Merriam-Webster, and every standard etymology dictionary confirm this derivation. The word for betraying your country entered English from the name for Christians who handed over sacred texts to save their lives under Roman persecution. The burning of scriptures is the source of the word.
Primary source: Lactantius, De Mortibus Persecutorum (~315 CE), describes the Diocletianic edicts in detail. Lactantius was a Christian scholar who survived the persecution and wrote the immediate aftermath. Not a neutral source — but the edicts themselves are confirmed by Eusebius and by archaeological evidence (destroyed church foundations from this period).
Honest limit: The degree to which the persecution was uniformly enforced across the empire varied by region and local governor. Some areas experienced mass martyrdom; others were relatively untouched. The policy was empire-wide. The execution was not.

6. The Council of Nicaea — Who Was in Charge

325 CE. Emperor Constantine convenes approximately 300 bishops from across the empire at Nicaea (modern Iznik, Turkey). The question on the table: was Christ co-eternal with God (Nicene position) or was he created by God (Arian position)?
“When all were seated, there was silence for a little while, while each awaited the arrival of the others. Then, when word spread that the emperor was approaching, all rose. At last he entered — resplendent in purple and gold, gleaming as it were with rays of light, very tall, outshining all around him in his majestic bearing.” — Eusebius of Caesarea, Life of Constantine 3.10. Eusebius was there. He describes Constantine arriving in imperial regalia and sitting in a golden throne at the center of the assembly.
Primary source — the unbaptized detail: Constantine was not baptized until he was on his deathbed, ~337 CE (Eusebius, Life of Constantine 4.61–62). In the theology he was presiding over, an unbaptized person is a catechumen — not yet fully Christian. By the church’s own doctrine, the man who called the council, sat at its head, and effectively determined the outcome of the Arian controversy was not a member of the institution whose doctrine he was writing.
Framework reading: The Arian position (Christ is created, not co-eternal) was dominant in the Eastern Empire — precisely the regions Constantine most needed to pacify. Arianism threatened religious unity, and therefore imperial unity. Constantine’s motive for supporting the Nicene (anti-Arian) position is not documented explicitly, but the political logic is plain. A unified Christianity is a useful instrument of imperial control. A fragmented one isn’t.
Primary source — what Nicaea actually decided: The Nicene Creed was written at this council. It remains the doctrinal statement of most Christian denominations today. It was written by an assembly called by a Roman emperor, presided over by a Roman emperor, in a city in the Roman Empire. The creed opens: “I believe in one God, the Father Almighty...” The word “Almighty” translates pantokrator — a title previously used for Roman emperors.
Honest limit: Constantine’s personal faith is unknowable. His conversion may have been genuine. His political interests and his theological positions may have genuinely aligned. We cannot read his inner state. What is documented: his political actions, his unbaptized status, and the outcomes of the council. We report the actions, not the soul.

8. The Cross

Roman crucifixion. The method reserved for slaves and seditionists. The most degrading public death Rome could administer. The bodies of the crucified were typically left to rot as a warning. This is what they did to him.
Primary source: The cross as Christian symbol is absent from the first three centuries of Christian art. The earliest surviving unambiguous depiction of the crucifixion in Christian art is the 5th-century ivory panel in the British Museum (~420 CE) and the wooden doors of Santa Sabina, Rome (~432 CE). Prior to Constantine, Christians used the fish, the anchor, the Chi-Rho. The cross as symbol appears and spreads after Constantine made Christianity politically safe. The shift is documented in the archaeological record of early Christian art.
Framework reading: The instrument of state execution became the symbol of the state religion. If a future empire executed the founder of a movement by electric chair, and 300 years later that empire made his movement mandatory and put the electric chair on their state buildings — we would note the irony. The cross is that irony, preserved in stone and gold for 1,700 years.
Honest limit: The meaning of the cross within Christian theology is complex and not reducible to irony. The theology of redemptive suffering transforms the execution instrument intentionally. The point here is not that the symbol is wrong — it’s that the symbol’s adoption was simultaneous with Roman imperial absorption of the movement. Cause and meaning are separate questions.

10. What Rome Couldn’t Kill

The signal survived the institution because it was never in the institution. It was distributed, peer to peer, person to person. It lived in small groups meeting in homes, sharing meals, singing, laying on hands, passing the story by voice. Rome could burn scriptures. It couldn’t burn a conversation.

The 300-year failure to suppress it is evidence of how it was structured. A centralized organization can be decapitated. A distributed network propagates around the kill. Every martyrdom created more converts than it eliminated. This is documented in Tertullian (~197 CE): “The blood of the martyrs is the seed of the church.” He was describing something he observed empirically, not making theology.

And then — because the network survived — the empire had to absorb it. You cannot absorb something that doesn’t exist. The absorption of 325–380 CE is proof that the thing being absorbed was real and alive. Rome did not invent Christianity. Rome captured it. The capture is what this page is about.

Honest limits — what we won’t claim

× We don’t know Constantine’s inner motives. His conversion may have been sincere. We document actions, not souls.

× We don’t claim Nicaea “invented” the Bible. The canon was not formally fixed at Nicaea — that’s a popular misconception. The canon developed over the 4th and 5th centuries. What Nicaea did was establish the authority of councils to make such decisions, and define the Trinitarian doctrine.

× We don’t claim individual Christians’ experiences aren’t real. The institution captured the signal. People kept receiving it anyway. That’s the point of the distributed network.

× We don’t claim the message was wrong. The opposite. The message was strong enough that 280 years of state violence couldn’t kill it. We’re saying what happened to the institution around it.

× Caesarea Maritima’s harbor was built by Herod the Great, ~22–10 BCE — before Jesus. It’s a Roman-era building but not a post-execution construction. We correct this before it can be used against the argument.
