Mutation Scanner

Variant pathogenicity scoring — 1,594 variants across 15 disease proteins
JIM’S OVERSIMPLIFICATION

Some amino acids are bridges holding two parts of a protein together. Mutate the bridge, the protein falls apart. We scan for bridges. If your mutation hits one, it matters. If it doesn’t, it probably doesn’t.

WHAT THIS DOES

You have a genetic mutation. You want to know: does it matter? Is it going to make someone sick? Or is it just a harmless spelling change in the DNA? This question gets asked millions of times a year in hospitals and genetics labs.

The mutation scanner answers it by asking two things. First: how structurally important is this position? If you remove this amino acid, does the protein's connectivity collapse? (That is the Fiedler damage score — it treats the protein like a network and measures how much the network falls apart when you pull one node.) Second: has evolution preserved this position? If every species from fish to humans has the same amino acid here, it is probably important.

HOW WELL IT WORKS

0.74 AUC across 6 disease proteins, tested under the strictest possible validation (leave-one-gene-out). That matches SIFT, the field's classic tool from 2001. It does not match AlphaMissense (0.94 AUC), which was trained on 100 million sequences. We use zero training data. Zero. The value is not raw accuracy — it is that every score is traceable to a physical mechanism. You can see WHY a mutation is scored as damaging, not just that it is.

The novel feature: Fiedler network damage. It measures how much the protein's connectivity suffers when you remove a residue. As a single metric, it achieves 0.82 AUC. One number. No training. Pure graph theory on the protein's 3D contact map.

WHAT IT CATCHES

KRAS G12D (pancreatic cancer's most famous mutation). p53 R175H (the single most common cancer mutation in all humans). Sickle cell disease (HBB E6V). SOD1 A4V (ALS). It also correctly identifies common benign variants — p53 P72R is carried by 72% of the population and scores exactly zero. 216,000 variants per second using precomputed lookup tables.

THE IDEA

A protein mutation is pathogenic when it disrupts a position that matters. Two questions determine this: how coupled is the position to its neighbors (contact degree from 3D structure), and how conserved is it across species (evolutionary memory from ortholog alignment). If both are high, the position is load-bearing and the mutation is damaging.

Scoring signals as coupled features. Edge weight = correlation strength. Hover to explore dependencies.

Population frequency is the third signal. If a variant exists at ≥1% frequency in healthy humans, it is not causing Mendelian disease. gnomAD provides this data for 807,000 exomes. We index it locally — 18,272 variants across 15 genes, zero API calls.

THE NUMBERS

Tested against 1,594 ClinVar missense variants across 6 disease proteins (p53, AR, BRAF, EGFR, RET, MTHFR), plus 63 ClinVar-reviewed benign variants. Evaluated under leave-one-gene-out cross-validation — the strictest test of generalization.

Regime-aware scorer (Fiedler damage × functional proximity × substitution × environment):
Within-gene AUC: 0.74 (mean across 6 proteins, leave-one-gene-out)
Two regimes: LoF genes use structural damage. GoF genes use MSA tolerance.

Per-gene (within-protein discrimination):
EGFR: 0.92  |  RET: 0.85  |  BRAF: 0.75  |  p53: 0.66  |  MTHFR: 0.63  |  AR: 0.60

Novel structural feature:
Fiedler damage (weighted-graph algebraic connectivity perturbation): 0.82 AUC as a single feature under LOGO cross-validation. Measures global network vulnerability — how much the protein’s connectivity suffers when a residue is removed.

Scoring speed:
Online (per-variant): 30 variants/sec
Precomputed lookup tables: 216,211 variants/sec (6,975x faster)

Within-gene AUC measures discrimination between pathogenic and benign variants within the same protein — the honest test that avoids cross-gene confounders. Previous claims (94.7%, 83.4%) were inflated by gene-level confounders and in-sample weight optimization, discovered and corrected in Session 23 (April 15, 2026). The current 0.74 is validated under strict leave-one-gene-out cross-validation with no learned weights.

GUMP reports within-gene AUC (discrimination within each protein), not cross-gene balanced accuracy. Within-gene AUC is the harder, more honest metric — it tests whether the scorer can distinguish pathogenic from benign at the same structural locus, not just between different proteins. GUMP matches SIFT with zero training and a fully interpretable mechanism panel. The gap to AlphaMissense (0.94) reflects 100M learned sequence embeddings vs physics-only features. GUMP’s value is interpretability and speed, not raw AUC.

KNOWN LIMITS

Irreducible from sequence alone:
  Gain-of-function — protein works too well, not too poorly
  DNA-contact mutations — fold intact, binding surface damaged
  Tetramer destabilization — needs inter-molecular geometry
  Epistasis — compensatory mutations change the coupling landscape

Addressable with more data:
  AR ligand-binding domain — needs deeper ortholog sets
  Rare benign variants without gnomAD entry — needs larger population databases

THE SIGNALS

Verdict (2 signals):
  1. K — contact degree at position (size-normalized)
  2. Conservation — BLOSUM62 ortholog alignment (61–157 species)
  Filter: gnomAD population frequency (AF ≥ 1% → benign)

Mechanism analysis (15 signals, for drug strategy):
  Surface patches, charge networks, aggregation, flexibility,
  secondary structure, metal binding, catalytic sites,
  packing, salt bridges, burial, terminal interfaces
