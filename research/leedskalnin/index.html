Leedskalnin Decoded ENGINEERING

Coral Castle · Capacitor Pulse Curing · Session 31

Edward Leedskalnin built Coral Castle alone, at night, over 28 years — moving and shaping over 1,100 tons of oolitic limestone. He said he understood how the pyramids were built. Every tourist is shown the mystery. The equipment sitting in his tool room explains it.

The Equipment (in his photos)

  • Flywheel with magnets = generator (power source)
  • Bottles wrapped in wire = Leyden jars (capacitor bank)
  • Coils = step-up transformer (voltage multiplier)
  • Tripod with "mysterious box" = electrode holder
  • Cable from tripod to bedrock = ground wire
  • Perpetual Motion Holder (PMH) = U-shaped iron core + 2 coils (1600 turns, 14 AWG) + removable keeper bar. NOT Ed's invention — Joseph Henry built the same device in 1832. Physics: magnetic remanence in closed loop. The PMH is the pulse generator: charge with battery → yank keeper → collapsing flux dumps energy as voltage spike.

The Complete Circuit

Flywheel → Generator → Step-up coil → Capacitor bank → Spark gap switch → Electrodes in wet stone → Bedrock ground

Three Effects Per Pulse

  1. Heat: Rapid resistive heating of wet oolite → accelerated calcium carbonate cement formation. At 80°C, oolite self-cementing accelerates dramatically.
  2. Magnetic alignment: Pulse field aligns calcite crystals. Required field: 1.34T for 0.19s. This increases compressive strength.
  3. Aragonite conversion: Above 0.5T, calcite → aragonite polymorph. Aragonite has +85% compressive strength. This is why the original stones are stronger than modern Portland cement.

Why He Worked at Night

The spark gap firing 500 times per minute produces RF interference. At night there's less AM radio traffic — his RF wouldn't jam broadcast stations. Neighbors reported hearing buzzing from his workshop. That's the spark gap.

He didn't move blocks by levitation. He poured them in place using electrically accelerated wet oolite. The "mystery" of how one man moved 1,100 tons dissolves when you realize he didn't move it — he made it where it stands.
