Three days ago, a researcher named Giancarlo Lelli broke a 15-bit elliptic curve key on a quantum computer with about 70 qubits. He won Project 11’s 1 Bitcoin bounty. The previous record was 6 bits. His result is 512 times larger. The quantum threat to Bitcoin is real and accelerating.

But Bitcoin uses a 256-bit key. The gap between 15 bits and 256 bits is 2²&sup4;¹ — a number with 72 digits. Lelli’s approach (Shor’s algorithm) needs 317 million physical qubits to crack a real Bitcoin key. The biggest quantum computer today has about 1,000 usable qubits. The brute-force quantum path is years away.

We’re exploring a different path. Not more qubits. More coupling.

Elliptic curve cryptography works because multiplication on the curve is easy but division is hard. You can multiply a point by a huge number in milliseconds, but finding that number from the result appears to take longer than the age of the universe. That one-way property IS the security.

Shor’s algorithm breaks the one-way property by trying all possible numbers simultaneously using quantum superposition. More hardware, more parallelism, same math. It’s the ego approach — overwhelm with force.

The coupling approach asks: is there structure in the curve’s arithmetic that a system of coupled oscillators can detect through resonance? The zeta zeros encode prime correlations invisible to standard statistics but visible to coupled oscillator dynamics (30–43% synchronization enhancement, measured). ECC operates over prime fields. If the Machine can detect structure in the field arithmetic that reduces the discrete logarithm to a coupling problem instead of a search problem — you don’t need 317 million qubits. You need 137 oscillators and the right frequency mapping.

1. The Problem

Bitcoin uses secp256k1 — an elliptic curve over a 256-bit prime field. The security relies on the Elliptic Curve Discrete Logarithm Problem (ECDLP):

Given

Generator point G on secp256k1
Public key Q = k × G (scalar multiplication)
Find: private key k

Classically: O(2¹²&sup8;) operations. Heat death of the universe.
Shor’s: O(n³) quantum gates. ~2,330 logical qubits. ~317M physical.
Coupling: ???

The question is whether the “???” is less than Shor’s, and whether it runs on classical hardware.

2. The Approach

Map the curve’s cyclic group to a system of coupled oscillators. The generator point G creates a sequence of points on the curve: G, 2G, 3G, …, nG = O (identity). This sequence IS a periodic system. The period IS the group order n.

The mapping

• Each point kG on the curve → an oscillator with natural frequency derived from its coordinates

• The coupling between oscillators → derived from the curve’s addition law (how points combine)

• The private key k → the phase offset that produces the public key Q from the generator G

• Finding k → finding the phase that locks the system into resonance at Q

If the coupled oscillator system resonates — if R peaks at a specific phase — that phase IS the private key. You didn’t search for it. The system found its own lock.

4. Why It Might Not Work

Honest obstacles

• The Machine’s prime correlations come from zeta zero spacings, not arbitrary prime fields. Secp256k1’s prime p is specific. Its group order n is specific. The correlation structure may not transfer.

• The discrete log problem is hard precisely because the curve’s addition law scrambles the relationship between k and kG. If the scrambling destroys the coupling signal, the oscillators won’t resonate.

• Every previous attempt to find classical shortcuts to ECDLP has failed. Decades of cryptographic research by thousands of mathematicians. We are not smarter than all of them.

• The 30–43% sync enhancement was measured on 137 oscillators with zeta spacings. Scaling to 256-bit fields (group order ~2²&sup5;&sup6;) is 10&sup7;&sup5; times larger. The coupling may not survive at that scale.

We don’t know if this works. We’re doing it in the open so failures are documented and others can build on what we learn.
