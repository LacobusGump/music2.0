JIM’S OVERSIMPLIFICATION

Two drugs competing for the same parking spot. The stronger binder wins. The weaker one floats around with nowhere to go. That’s a drug interaction. Same coupling, competitive exclusion.

K IN THIS DOMAIN

K here is binding affinity. Two drugs competing for the same site = coupled displacement. The stronger binder wins. Edges flash when interactions conflict.

THE MODEL

Most drugs are metabolized by the CYP450 enzyme family in the liver. The interaction type determines the danger:

CYP450 Interaction Types

Inhibitor + prodrug on same CYP → prodrug never activates (score: 4.0)
Inhibitor + substrate on same CYP → substrate accumulates to toxic levels (score: 3.0)
Inducer + prodrug on same CYP → prodrug over-activated (score: 3.0)
Inducer + substrate on same CYP → substrate cleared too fast, sub-therapeutic (score: 2.5)
Inducer + inhibitor on same CYP → unpredictable competition (score: 2.0)
Two substrates on same CYP → mutual competition (score: 1.5)

7 CYP enzymes tracked:
  • CYP3A4 — 30 drugs (22 substrates, 6 inhibitors, 2 inducers)
  • CYP2D6 — 11 drugs (6 substrates, 3 inhibitors, 2 prodrugs)
  • CYP2C9 — 8 drugs (4 substrates, 3 inhibitors, 1 inducer)
  • CYP2C19 — 6 drugs (4 substrates, 1 inducer, 1 prodrug)
  • CYP1A2 — 4 drugs (3 substrates, 1 inhibitor)
  • CYP2B6 — 2 drugs (2 substrates)
  • CYP2E1 — 1 drug (acetaminophen)

Clinical risk multipliers:
  • QT prolongation (additive cardiac arrest risk) +2.0
  • Serotonin syndrome (dual serotonergic) +2.5
  • CNS depression (opioid + sedative) +2.0
  • Bleeding risk (dual anticoagulant) +1.5
  • Narrow therapeutic index → 1.3x score multiplier per drug

THE DEADLIEST TRIPLE

Sertraline + Warfarin + Amiodarone — Combined Score: 25.2

Three drugs. Three CYP enzymes blocked. 10 distinct mechanisms of danger.

  Amiodarone inhibits CYP3A4 → both sertraline and warfarin accumulate
  Amiodarone inhibits CYP2C9 → both sertraline and warfarin accumulate
  Amiodarone inhibits CYP2D6 → sertraline accumulates
  Sertraline and warfarin compete for CYP2C9
  Sertraline and warfarin compete for CYP3A4
  Two narrow therapeutic index drugs (warfarin + amiodarone)

The result:
  Warfarin accumulates → uncontrolled bleeding
  Sertraline accumulates through 3 pathways → serotonin toxicity
  Amiodarone baseline risk → QT prolongation, cardiac arrest

All three drugs are commonly prescribed. A cardiac patient on amiodarone
who also takes warfarin (blood thinner) and sertraline (depression).
This is a real patient profile. Score: 25.2.

COMMON COMBOS PEOPLE ACTUALLY TAKE

These are combinations prescribed together every day. Many patients are never warned.

The cardiac triple: warfarin + amiodarone + omeprazole. Heart patient on a blood thinner, an antiarrhythmic, and a stomach acid reducer. Amiodarone blocks warfarin clearance through CYP3A4 and CYP2C9. Warfarin blood levels rise. Bleeding risk climbs. Omeprazole adds CYP2C19 competition. This is a standard cardiology regimen.

The depression + pain combo: fluoxetine + tramadol. SSRI + opioid. Fluoxetine inhibits CYP2D6 → tramadol accumulates AND both are serotonergic → serotonin syndrome. Two mechanisms of danger. Often prescribed by different doctors who do not communicate.

The stent + stomach: omeprazole + clopidogrel. PPI for acid reflux + blood thinner after stent placement. CYP2C19: omeprazole is a substrate, clopidogrel is a prodrug. Omeprazole blocks clopidogrel activation. The stent protection fails. FDA has issued warnings. Still routinely co-prescribed.

The breast cancer trap: fluoxetine + tamoxifen. Fluoxetine inhibits CYP2D6. Tamoxifen is a CYP2D6 prodrug — it must be activated by CYP2D6 to work. Fluoxetine blocks the activation. The cancer drug stops working. Score: 5.2. Published, known, still prescribed together.

The transplant patient: cyclosporine + ketoconazole. Both go through CYP3A4. Ketoconazole is a strong inhibitor. Cyclosporine is a narrow therapeutic index substrate. Cyclosporine blood levels spike → nephrotoxicity. Score: 5.85.

HONEST LIMITS

What this model does NOT do:

  • CYP data from FDA labels. Pairwise only. No pharmacokinetic modeling.
  • No dose-response curves. A 5mg statin is scored the same as 80mg.
  • No patient-specific CYP polymorphism prediction (CYP2D6 poor metabolizers, etc.)
  • No P-glycoprotein interactions (digoxin, etc.) beyond clinical flags
  • No Phase II metabolism (glucuronidation, sulfation, acetylation)
  • No protein binding displacement interactions
  • No food interactions (grapefruit juice = CYP3A4 inhibitor in the gut)
  • No renal/hepatic impairment adjustment
  • 49 drugs is ~10% of commonly prescribed medications

What it IS:
  A structural screen. If two drugs share a CYP enzyme, this model tells you
  which one will accumulate and why. That is the first question any pharmacist asks.
  This answers it in milliseconds for all 1,176 pairs.

This is computational research, not medical advice. Drug interactions depend on individual genetics (CYP polymorphisms), dosing, renal/hepatic function, and other factors not captured here. Always consult a pharmacist or physician before changing medications. This model identifies enzyme competition risk from published metabolic profiles.
