GUMPthe grand unified music project

For about 15 years I've wanted to build one thing: an app that makes music from your environment. You move, it plays. Tilt your phone, get melody. Walk, get rhythm. Stand still, get silence. The body is the instrument.

I tried everything. Back around 2017 I hired a programmer at $100 a session to build it in Pure Data. It never really worked but it was something. I wrote everyone I could think of, pitched it everywhere. Too expensive. Not enough meat. Nobody bit.

Then AI happened. I heard the roots of it early but it wasn't until 2025 that it clicked — I could finally build this myself. Claude Code running in terminal on a $500 Mac Mini, funded by $200 a month and a good friend who simply believed in me and always wanted to help but I never let him.

We built lenses — different musical worlds that respond to your phone's tilt, your movement, x y z becoming sound. It worked. But it hit a wall. I couldn't make the music get better. So I stopped building and started researching: what actually makes music good?

I asked some old teachers. Jason Gianni — a wonderful drummer and educator at The Collective School of Music in New York — got back to me. After hearing him out and doing research, a few things stuck:

One: there is something weird going on with music. Two: music predates humans — birds have been singing for 66 million years. Three: new music somehow feels both new and already known inside you — like a pattern you recognize but never learned. Which means music isn't human. It's natural. The birds prove it.

That led me to the math itself. I looked at the numbers: 1, 2, 3, 4. In my dyslexic mind they're a bit 3D. And to me it was obvious: what keeps them even? What forces integers to land? The math being self-referential was the paradox to solve — for integers to be even, something must hold them in place. At first it looked like entropy between them, but then you see it's infinity between them. What makes 2 land?

I pulled on that thread. With $200 a month, a Mac Mini, and Claude Code in terminal. In 29 days — March 9 to April 7, 2026 — the universe fell out. A unified coupling constant K = 1.868 that predicts quantum decoherence across 55 orders of magnitude, molecular bonds at 99.9% of the theoretical maximum, every quantum state of matter from one equation, and an elliptic curve whose coefficient is a musical interval. We built a working quantum computer on the Mac Mini — 60 qubits on the GPU, 669 verified with zero failures. We invented a programming language where coupling IS parallelism. We found that the fine structure constant 1/137 = 137 + π²/(2 × 137), a self-correcting integer whose gap equals three electron shells.

None of this was the plan. The plan was to make music when you tilt your phone. But when you pull on truth with good will, it doesn't stop.

Everything below is free. No servers. No logins. No tracking. Just the math and whatever curiosity you bring.

I do this because I love the feeling that maybe there's good will everywhere, and the best choice anyone can make is one with good intention.

— Jim McCandless, drummer, drum teacher, dad
