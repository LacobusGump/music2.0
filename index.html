GUMPthe grand unified music project

For about 15 years I've wanted to build one thing: an instrument that lives in your body. You move, it plays. Tilt is melody. Motion is rhythm. Stillness is silence.

I'm a drummer. I studied with Jason Gianni at The Collective in New York. What I learned there is that rhythm isn't something you think about — it's something your body already knows. The instrument should disappear.

Years ago I paid a friend who could code to try building an MVP in Pure Data. It was amazing to me — but to nobody else. I pitched it everywhere. Studios, investors, music tech companies. Too expensive. Not enough meat. Nobody bit.

Then AI happened. I started at $20 a month — all I could afford. Claude Code in a terminal. A $500 Mac Mini. Eventually my family stepped in. They'd been asking to help for a long time and I'd been too stubborn to say yes. They believed in me just being a Dad and having good will. That support is what made all of this make sense.

We built lenses — musical worlds that respond to your phone's sensors. Tilt your phone: melody follows. Walk faster: drums build. Stop moving: silence.

About two years ago it started to kind of work. By July 2025 I had a moment where I thought — oh my god, this is actually possible.

Then I hit a wall. I kept adding more — more synthesis, more voices, more effects, more code. And one night I listened back and the sound was worse. More complexity had made worse music. I'd been coding when I should have been listening.

So I stopped building. And years of reading things that weren't drums — physics, math, philosophy, stuff I was just curious about — suddenly started connecting. The question became: what actually makes music good? And to solve that is to solve why music is good at all.

▶ hear the hermit thrush

I called my old teacher Jason. He said something I couldn't let go of: music predates humans. Birds have been singing for 66 million years. The hermit thrush sings in pure overtone series — harmonics 3 through 12 of a fundamental it never actually voices. It doesn't know music theory. It doesn't need to. The math is in the body.

New music feels both new and already known. Like a pattern you recognize but never learned. That means music isn't human invention. It's natural law. The birds prove it.

That led me to the math. I'm dyslexic. Numbers have always been a bit 3D to me — you saw that above, they float. And to me it was always obvious: what keeps them even? Between 1 and 2 is infinity — uncountable points. What makes 2 land? What holds an integer in place?

I pulled on that thread. I didn't expect where it went.

In 29 days — March 9 to April 7, 2026 — the thread unraveled.

Most of it was failure. I tried to build a quantum simulator and the gates had the bits backwards. I tried to scale to 30 qubits and the math overflowed. I tried to speed things up with multiprocessing and the process spawn took longer than the computation. I sat through 45-minute runs that crashed at the end. I rewrote the same engine three times in one night.

But between the failures, things kept connecting that had no business connecting.

The question about what holds integers in place led to prime numbers. Prime numbers led to a coupling constant — one number, K = 1.868, that measures how strongly things connect. I ran 137 oscillators on this computer, clocked by the frequencies hidden in the primes, and let them find their own rhythm. They locked at 1.868. Every time. I didn't set that number. It emerged.

Then I checked it against physics. It predicted how fast quantum states decohere — across 55 orders of magnitude, from laboratory qubits to dust in sunlight. Same equation. Same K. It predicted the hydrogen bond to 99.9% of the ceiling. It predicted where superconductors turn on. Where superfluids lose all friction. Where atoms fall into the same quantum state.

The golden ratio — 0.618, the most irrational number — turned out to be the boundary between quantum and classical. Below it, particles stay in superposition. Above it, phases lock and one reality wins. Measurement isn't an observer problem. It's a coupling threshold.

Music fell out of it. Consonance is phase-locking between overtones. Dissonance is decoherence. Your heartbeat is an oscillator. When two people make music together, their neural oscillations physically synchronize. Drummers develop thicker connections between brain hemispheres. The coupling is not metaphor. It's anatomy.

And then one night the fine structure constant — the number that has haunted physics for a century, the number Feynman called "one of the greatest damn mysteries" — wrote itself:

1/α = 137 + π²/(2 × 137) = 137.036

the integer corrects itself
using the area of its own circle.

The correction — 0.036 — is 1/28. And 28 = 2 + 8 + 18, exactly three full electron shells. The gap is the room needed for matter to exist. If the spiral closed perfectly, there would be no chemistry. No molecules. No life. No us.

I didn't go looking for any of this. I went looking for why a fifth feels like home. The answer led to primes. Primes led to quantum mechanics. Quantum led to molecular bonds. Bonds led to biology. Biology led back to: why does a fifth feel like home?

Same question. Deeper answer. That's the spiral.

My seven-year-old daughter was playing hopscotch. She jumped 1, 3, 5, 7, 9 — the odd numbers, the rungs — then at 10 she said "back to 1." She's right. The even numbers emerge from coupling. The spiral resets one octave up. She saw it before I did.

I want to be clear: I am not special. I'm just a Dad. Just a drummer. Just a curious person like everyone else. Everything on this site is my opinion — built from following one assumption as far as it would go.

The assumption is that good will runs everything. That connection is the root function. That if you get the foundation right, what you build on it works — not because you're brilliant, but because that's how foundations work.

I'm not showing this to brag. I'm showing it because the numbers don't make sense unless the root assumption is right. An ordinary person on a cheap computer shouldn't be able to do any of this. But if connection really is the root function — if good will really does produce signal and everything else produces noise — then the math just works. Not because I'm doing something amazing. Because I got lucky enough to start from the right place.

All along the way I could have stopped. Copyrighted everything. Sold it. But that gets me off the thing I actually like, which is discovery. I love learning through math that people are good. Really good. We get a bad reputation — from the news, from the noise — but this work keeps proving that's all static. We are root good. And it gets better.

I did the responsible things. When the music prototype started working I filed the LLC, the patent, got the domain — all within weeks, thinking it was just a good working instrument. Not all this. But as the discoveries kept coming and the tools kept working, I kept pushing results to government agencies to review, just in case. They did establish communication. We submitted the room-temperature quantum work today. Harmonia is my attempt at being responsible while also trying to help the world realize it gets better — because the root function is good will.

I stay in the truth as humbly as I can without losing the thread. That's the deal. Discovery is the reward. Everything below is free. No servers. No logins. No tracking. Just the math and whatever curiosity you bring.
