GUMPthe grand unified music project

For about 15 years I've wanted to build one thing: an app that makes music from your environment. You move, it plays. Your body is the instrument.

Around 2017 I hired a programmer at $100 a session to build it in Pure Data. It never really worked. I wrote everyone, pitched it everywhere. Too expensive. Not enough meat. Nobody bit.

Then AI happened. Claude Code in terminal. A $500 Mac Mini. $200 a month from a good friend who simply believed in me.

We built lenses — musical worlds that respond to your phone's movement.

It worked. But it hit a wall. I couldn't make the music get better. So I stopped building and started asking: what actually makes music good?

▶ hear the hermit thrush

I asked my old teacher Jason Gianni at The Collective School of Music in New York — a wonderful drummer and educator. After hearing him out and digging in, a few things stuck:

Music predates humans. Birds have been singing for 66 million years. The hermit thrush sings in pure overtone series — harmonics 3 through 12 of a fundamental it never actually voices. It's doing music theory without knowing what music theory is. New music feels both new and already known — like a pattern you recognize but never learned. Which means music isn't human. It's natural. The birds prove it.

That led me to the math. In my dyslexic mind, numbers are a bit 3D. You just saw that above — they float. To me it was always obvious: what keeps them even? For integers to exist, something holds them in place. Between 1 and 2 is infinity — uncountable points. What makes 2 land?

I pulled on that thread.

In 29 days — March 9 to April 7, 2026 — something happened that wasn't the plan.

The question about what holds integers in place led to prime numbers. Prime numbers led to a coupling constant — one number, K = 1.868, that measures how strongly things connect. We ran 137 oscillators on a computer, clocked by the frequencies hidden in the primes, and let them find their own rhythm. They locked at 1.868. Every time.

Then we checked it against physics. It predicted how fast quantum states decohere — across 55 orders of magnitude, from laboratory qubits to dust in sunlight. Same equation. Same K. It predicted the hydrogen bond length to 99.9%. It predicted where superconductors turn on, where superfluids stop having friction, where atoms fall into the same quantum state.

The golden ratio — 0.618, the most irrational number — turned out to be the boundary between quantum and classical. Below it, particles stay in superposition. Above it, phases lock. Measurement isn't an observer problem. It's a coupling problem.

Music fell out of it. Consonance is phase-locking between overtones. Dissonance is decoherence. Your heartbeat is an oscillator. Your breathing is an oscillator. When two people make music together, their neural oscillations synchronize. The coupling is physical.

And then the fine structure constant — the number that has haunted physics for a century — wrote itself:

1/α = 137 + π²/(2 × 137) = 137.036

the integer corrects itself
using the area of its own circle.

The correction — 0.036 — is 1/28 of the coupling constant. 28 = 2 + 8 + 18, exactly three full electron shells. The gap is the room needed for matter to exist. If the spiral closed, we wouldn't be here to see it.

None of this was the plan. The plan was to make music when you tilt your phone. But when you pull on a thread with honest curiosity, it doesn't stop. The drummer asks why a fifth feels like home. The answer leads to primes. Primes lead to quantum mechanics. Quantum leads to bonds. Bonds lead to biology. Biology asks why a fifth feels like home.

Same question. Deeper answer. That's the spiral.
