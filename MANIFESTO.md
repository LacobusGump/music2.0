The future is always going to make you feel dumb, so use it.
Solve for the paradox of intention, against the hubris of self.

Intention may be subjective, like Einstein's relativity maps out. This makes outcomes also subjective. There is no prize but the "status of now" relative to all possible outcomes. (Sorry for how crunchy that line is, I'm trying to reduce word count.)

"1hr with your hand on the stove...1hr with the love of your life." That is where I am coming from, or nothing else I write has roots you can pull at.

What I learned, that actually works within the code, is don't frame your intentions as "make choices." Enable outcomes that produce the desired effects, then simplify it.

For me, it's great music. My first action after product, patent, LLC is research. "What makes music good? Rules like rule of 3s, Picardy thirds, circle of 5ths? Or, the artist is a vessel so no one will ever know?" I can solve for both but need to know where to go down.

MOST problems require "enabling outcomes" because you never have everything understood. We can't see them coming but black swans sing so I practice dancing.

Applied: Waking up on a fan's couch after asking the crowd for a bed because we couldn't afford a hotel in a country where we don't speak the language; is a better outcome (lived experience) than some passive-inflow-enabling 401k update when you hit your 30s (shoutout Mike Green for ruining my love of finance, I owe you big time).

You either have a spark in the eye still, or it's gone. That's the outcome vs the intention. I think our products are the same — does it give a spark or does it take it from another intention, ruining the path. Demand is always there, people pay to be ruined all the time because we confuse the intention for the outcome. I may be doing it right now, I should be getting black ink from Walmart to print documents but instead I'm answering questions no one asked.

"Real worth" is incredibly hard to conceptualize in a world stitched together with myths. Vectoring your strengths the wrong way spins you out. Compounding intentions generate exponential curves that are hard to turn.

Every second/cent I spend is technically and literally opportunity cost as what I prioritize is not optimal, never will be, and forever demand a need for iteration. I try to avoid paralysis analysis by accepting that no matter what choice I make, AT BEST, is 70% right and +10% better. Send it.

The good work, I *think*, is solving the paradox of intention against the hubris of self. To do that, it's using intentionality and respecting its power.
